# Case Study: Secondary School Workshop

## Context

**Participants:** Secondary school students (ages 14-16)
**Language:** Cantonese and English
**Duration:** 90-minute workshop
**Setting:** Classroom with 30 students

## The Challenge

Students had to prepare an oral presentation for an upcoming school event. Most had never been taught how to structure a story — they had "content" (what happened) but no "story" (why it matters).

## The Approach

We adapted the StoryCraft Framework for a shorter attention span, using the 3-stage version:

1. **Hook (15 min)** — LSP warm-up: "Build the ending first." Each student built a LEGO model of their story's ending point. Then we asked: "Now build where you started." The physical gap between the two models was their hook.

2. **Shape (30 min)** — "The Three-Brick Story" exercise. Students wrote three sentences (one per act) on sticky notes. We arranged them on the wall as a physical arc. The act of standing up and placing their bricks on the arc made the structure tangible.

3. **Flesh + Judge (30 min)** — Pair practise. One minute each. Partner gave One Love / One Suggestion. Then switch.

4. **Land (15 min)** — Vocal warm-up (HAIL exercises, 90 seconds). Final 60-second delivery to partner.

## What Happened

- Students who said they "hated public speaking" delivered stories with genuine emotion
- The LEGO warm-up bypassed the "I don't know what to write" paralysis
- Cantonese stories had more circular structure; English stories were more linear. Students naturally code-switched their storytelling style.

## Key Takeaway

> "The best moment was seeing a quiet student — who never raises his hand — tell a story about his grandfather that made the whole class silent." — Classroom Teacher

## What We Learned

- **Physicality unlocks stories.** Writing from a blank page is harder than building from a physical model.
- **Language shapes structure.** Bilingual students already know different story shapes — they just haven't been taught to recognise them.
- **Peer feedback works better than teacher feedback.** When students gave each other One Love / One Suggestion, they listened more carefully than they would to a teacher.
