# Design Philosophy — The Thinking Behind the Storytelling Skill

This file explains why this skill exists, why its stages are ordered this way, and why specific frameworks were chosen over alternatives. It's written for humans, not AI agents. The SKILL.md contains only what the agent needs to execute; this document contains the reasoning.

---

## The Core Idea

This skill draws on thousands of years of storytelling wisdom — from Aristotle to modern-day practitioners. The frameworks themselves are not new. What's new is how they're sequenced, how they're taught, and how an AI Agent can deliver them as a guided learning system.

The wisdom is not mine. The design is — the sequence, the exercises, the prompts, the checks that turn a library of knowledge into a step-by-step practice session.

---

## Why This Order Matters

Most storytelling resources start with structure (Three Acts, Hero's Journey). Students dutifully outline their story — and then lose interest because they don't yet know *why* anyone should care.

**This design puts Hook first** because Hook-first produces better stories in less time. You can't build a structure until you know what gap you're trying to create.

The stages are not strictly linear in practice. A student who reaches Land and bombs their delivery often realises their Hook was wrong. A student in Flesh who can't find a metaphor often discovers their Shape was missing a character spine. The stages are a **diagnostic tool** — if one stage is painful, it's telling you something about the stage before.

## Why These Frameworks (And What Didn't Make the Cut)

### Stage 1: Hook

**Chosen:** Nancy Duarte's What Is / What Could Be gap — the most teachable tension framework. One axis, one exercise, immediate results.

**Rejected:** The Hero's Journey (Campbell) — brilliant for analysing *The Odyssey*, confusing for a 16-year-old writing about their summer. Classic "opening techniques" like starting with a quote or a question — too many options, no decision framework.

### Stage 2: Shape

**Chosen:** Aristotle's Three-Act Structure — 3,000 years old and still the best foundation. Maps to how humans naturally process stories: before → during → after.

**Rejected:** Truby's 22-step system (too many for beginners), Snyder's 15-beat sheet (designed for 90-minute films), Campbell's Hero's Journey (excellent for analysis, confusing for creation). The SCRR structure (Humm) is included as an alternative for shorter formats.

**Character spine:** Adapted from Andrew Stanton (Pixar) — the most memorable characters are driven by a single, often unconscious goal.

### Stage 3: Flesh

**Chosen:** Stanton's "two plus two" rule — give the audience half, let them complete the pattern. Diane Callahan's sensory substitution technique — replace emotional abstractions with concrete sensory details. Metaphor techniques from advertising — a good metaphor makes the unfamiliar feel familiar in under 5 seconds.

**Rejected:** Generic "show don't tell" advice without specific exercises. Writing-by-committees approaches.

### Stage 4: Judge

**Chosen:** Chris Anderson's single-idea and worth-sharing tests (catch 80% of problems). Brené Brown's vulnerability framework (distinguishes authentic connection from performance). Chip & Dan Heath's SUCCESs framework (comprehensive evaluation across six dimensions).

**Why all three:** They catch different kinds of problems. Anderson catches clarity. Brown catches authenticity. Heath catches memorability.

### Stage 5: Land

**Chosen:** Julian Treasure's HAIL framework — the most teachable delivery system: specific exercises that take 90 seconds, not vague advice. Celeste Headlee's listening framework — separates performance from connection. Charisma on Command's 3-Phase Emotional Journey — addresses flat delivery in well-structured stories.

**Rejected:** Generic "be confident" advice. Memorisation-based delivery systems.

## The Learning Arc

Here's what's happening to the person using this skill:

| Stage | What they feel | What's really happening |
|:------|:---------------|:------------------------|
| Before starting | Nervous, rushed, convinced they can't | Fear of the blank page |
| Stage 1: Hook | Relief — "I know where I'm going" | The ending removes anxiety |
| Stage 2: Shape | Confidence — "I can see the whole thing" | Structure creates safety |
| Stage 3: Flesh | Immersion — "This feels like a real story" | Sensory detail creates ownership |
| Stage 4: Judge | Discomfort — "I have to cut my favourite part" | Creative judgement is being built |
| Stage 5: Land | Readiness — "I can actually do this" | Vocal warm-up bridges private writing and public telling |

## What This Skill Is Not

- It is **not** a collection of everything I've read. It's a curated selection of what actually works in a guided learning context.
- It is **not** an original invention. I didn't invent these techniques. I chose them from everything that exists.
- It is **not** a reference manual. It must be followed in order. The exercises must be done, not read.
- It is **not** a replacement for creativity. It's a scaffold. Once you internalise the stages, you won't need the framework anymore.

---

## What This Skill Is

- A **guided learning system** — designed so an AI Agent can deliver it step by step
- A **curated library** — 25+ sources, filtered through a lens of creative judgement
- A **diagnostic tool** — if one stage is painful, it reveals a problem in the stage before
- A **bridge** between thousands of years of storytelling wisdom and the person who needs to tell a story right now

---

## Creative Appreciation and Judgement

The new generation has access to more creative tools than any before — AI that writes, generates, and produces at the push of a button. What they have less of is practice in creative appreciation and judgement: the ability to look at a story and know what's working, what's not, and why.

This is the muscle this skill exists to rebuild. Every exercise, every checklist, every rubric is designed not just to help someone *produce* a story, but to help them *judge* one — their own and others'. The frameworks were chosen because they develop that critical eye most efficiently.

In an age of infinite output, the ability to discern quality is the only durable advantage.
