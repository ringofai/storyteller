# Simulation: Masters Using the Storytelling Skill

A thought experiment — imagining how the world's top storytellers would use this skill with their AI Agent, and why even they would find it valuable.

---

## Nancy Duarte (Resonate, Presentation Genius)

### Who She Is
Author of *Resonate*. Analysed the greatest speeches in history (MLK, Jobs, Churchill) and discovered the What Is / What Could Be pattern. Her framework is the foundation of Stage 1: HOOK in this skill.

### What She'd Use It For
She coaches CEOs and TED speakers. Every client needs the same foundation — but she's tired of explaining the What Is / What Could Be gap for the thousandth time.

### The Simulation

> **Duarte's AI Agent:** "I have a new client — a CEO who needs to announce a restructuring. Walk him through the Hook stage."
>
> The agent loads the skill. Runs the 3-question hook exercise. The CEO writes his ending first ("This restructuring will save 200 jobs") and his current reality ("Everyone thinks I'm cutting jobs, not saving them"). The gap produces a hook in 2 minutes.
>
> **Duarte's reaction:** "That used to take me a 2-hour coaching session. Now it takes 2 minutes and I can focus on the nuance — the emotional pacing, the delivery, the Q&A. The skill handles the structure. I handle the craft."

### Why She Values It
*"I've spent 20 years teaching people this exact framework. Now I can hand the fundamentals to an AI Agent and spend my time where I actually add value — the moments the framework can't predict."*

## Andrew Stanton (Pixar — Toy Story, WALL-E, Finding Nemo)

### Who He Is
Writer/director at Pixar. Gave the TED talk "The Clues to a Great Story" — 6 clues that every story needs. His character spine technique and "two plus two" rule are foundational to Stages 2 and 3 of this skill.

### What He'd Use It For
He runs story sessions with new Pixar writers. The problem: junior writers know theory but freeze when asked to apply it.

### The Simulation

> **Stanton's AI Agent:** "I have a junior writer with a solid concept but no structure. Run Stage 2 — but make sure you hit the character spine first."
>
> The agent runs the character spine exercise before the three-act outline. The writer answers: "My hero wants to be left alone more than anything." The agent pauses: "If they got left alone, would the story end?" The writer realises — yes, the story would end. That's not a hero; that's a passive protagonist. The writer needs to find a hero who wants something that, if achieved, would NOT end the story.
>
> **Stanton's reaction:** "That's exactly the question I ask in every story meeting. It took me 20 years to learn to ask it. Now an AI Agent asks it on my behalf before I even walk into the room."

### Why He Values It
*"The hardest part of teaching storytelling isn't explaining the principles. It's getting people to apply them before they've internalised them. This skill makes the principles executable — which is exactly what I spend my days trying to do."*

## Brené Brown (Vulnerability, Connection, TED's Most-Watched)

### Who She Is
Research professor. Spent 20 years studying vulnerability, courage, and connection. Her TED talk "The Power of Vulnerability" has 60+ million views. Her work on authentic vulnerability is central to Stage 4: JUDGE.

### What She'd Use It For
She trains leaders and organisations. The most common problem: people add performative vulnerability to their stories — they "put in a vulnerable moment" because they think it's a technique. It's not. It's a truth you reveal or you don't.

### The Simulation

> **Brown's AI Agent:** "A leader is writing a talk about failure. She's included a story about missing her daughter's recital. Run the vulnerability check."
>
> The agent runs the check: "Is there a moment that makes you uncomfortable sharing it?" The leader says: "No, I'm comfortable sharing it." The agent flags: "If you're completely comfortable, you're probably not sharing enough. The discomfort is the signal that you've hit something real. Look deeper — what's the detail you're avoiding?"
>
> **Brown's reaction:** "That's the most important coaching question I ask, and it's embedded in a checklist that any agent can run. This doesn't replace the human work of vulnerability. It clears the path to it."

### Why She Values It
*"Most of my coaching time is spent undoing what bad storytelling advice did. People add vulnerability because someone told them to. This skill doesn't tell them to add it — it helps them find whether it's already there. That's the difference between performance and truth."*

## Simon Sinek (Start with Why, Golden Circle)

### Who He Is
Author of *Start with Why*. His Golden Circle (Why → How → What) is used in Stage 2: SHAPE of this skill. His TED talk "How Great Leaders Inspire Action" has 65+ million views.

### What He'd Use It For
He consults with organisations trying to find their Why. The problem: people confuse their What (products, services) with their Why (purpose, cause, belief). The Golden Circle is simple to understand and hard to apply.

### The Simulation

> **Sinek's AI Agent:** "A startup founder is pitching investors. She has the What (a fintech app) and the How (AI-powered budgeting). She's stuck on the Why. Run the Golden Circle exercise."
>
> The agent runs: "Communicate from the inside out. What do you BELIEVE?" The founder says: "I believe people should feel in control of their money, not controlled by it." The agent: "That's your Why. Everything else supports it." The agent structures her three acts around that Why — not the app features, but the belief.
>
> **Sinek's reaction:** "The Golden Circle is deceptively simple. People nod along and then produce a Why that's just their mission statement rewritten. This skill forces them to actually do the work — and the AI Agent can run the exercise with anyone, anywhere, without me in the room."

### Why He Values It
*"I've given this framework to millions of people. Most of them leave it on the shelf because applying it is harder than understanding it. This skill makes it executable. The Why ceases to be a concept and becomes a practice."*

## Julian Treasure (HAIL Framework, Vocal Delivery)

### Who He Is
Sound and communication expert. His TED talk "How to Speak So People Want to Listen" has 60+ million views. His HAIL framework and vocal toolbox are the foundation of Stage 5: LAND.

### What He'd Use It For
He coaches speakers on vocal delivery. His biggest frustration: people focus on what to say and ignore how to say it. They have a great story and a flat delivery.

### The Simulation

> **Treasure's AI Agent:** "A client has a story that works on paper but falls flat in delivery. Run the Land stage — live format."
>
> The agent runs the vocal warm-up (the exact 6 exercises Treasure teaches). Then coaches: "Mark 2 moments for silence of 2+ seconds. Where does the story need the audience to sit with what was just said?" The client marks two places. The agent continues: "Read it again. This time, at those two moments, count to three before continuing." The client does. The second version has weight the first didn't.
>
> **Treasure's reaction:** "The most common feedback I give is 'slow down and pause.' It's the simplest instruction and the hardest to follow. By making it a specific, measurable checkpoint — 'mark 2+ moments for silence' — this skill turns vague advice into a concrete practice."

### Why He Values It
*"I've spent 20 years teaching people that how you speak matters as much as what you say. This skill doesn't let people skip delivery because they're tired of writing. It makes delivery a non-negotiable stage with specific, repeatable exercises."*

## Common Thread: Why Masters Use This

Every master saw the same pattern: their framework was being consumed but not *applied*. People understood the theory but couldn't execute without a coach in the room.

The storytelling skill doesn't replace the master. It makes the master's wisdom executable at scale. The master handles the depth, the nuance, the edge cases. The skill handles the fundamentals — reliably, consistently, on demand.

As Stanton said: *"The hardest part of teaching storytelling isn't explaining the principles. It's getting people to apply them before they've internalised them."*

The skill closes that gap.
