# Storyteller Foundations — Brain Science & Stickiness

This module provides two essential layers that underpin the entire framework: **why stories work in the brain**, and **how to make stories stick in memory**. Teach this as a prelude to the 5-stage framework, or integrate the tools into relevant stages.

---

## Foundation 1: Why Stories Work — The Neuroscience Layer

> Based on *Wired for Story* (Lisa Cron) and *The Science of Storytelling* (Will Storr)

The brain is not a logic processor — it's a **prediction engine**. Stories work because they exploit the brain's default operating system.

### Three Neuroscience Principles Every Storyteller Needs

| Principle | What It Means | Classroom Application |
|:----------|:--------------|:---------------------|
| **Prediction Error** | The brain pays attention when reality doesn't match expectations. Tension = cognitive gap. | Start a story with an unexpected detail. The brain *has* to resolve the gap. "I walked into the room and everyone was silent. That's when I knew something was wrong." |
| **Causal Urgency** | The brain evolved to track cause-and-effect chains. It craves "what happens next?" | Every scene must answer the question from the previous scene. Test: "If someone read only the previous paragraph, would they NEED to read this one?" |
| **Identity as Narrative** | The brain constructs selfhood as a story. We don't just tell stories — we *are* stories. | Help students see their own experiences as narratives with arcs, conflicts, and resolutions. "What's the story you tell yourself about who you are?" |

### The "Sacred Flaw" Tool

> From *The Science of Storytelling* (Will Storr)

Every compelling protagonist has a **deeply held but wrong belief** — a "sacred flaw." The story's job is to challenge, complicate, or shatter that belief.

**Exercise:** Ask students: "What does your protagonist believe about themselves that isn't true?" If the answer is "nothing," the character isn't ready.

| Story | Protagonist | Sacred Flaw | What Shatters It |
|:------|:------------|:-------------|:-----------------|
| Finding Nemo | Marlin | "The ocean is dangerous; safety is everything." | Nemo's courage forces him to trust. |
| Toy Story | Woody | "My value comes from being Andy's favourite." | Buzz shows him belonging isn't rank. |

### The "First Sentence Three Questions" Check

> From *Wired for Story* (Lisa Cron)

Before writing the first sentence of any story, answer:
1. **Whose story is this?** (Who is the protagonist?)
2. **What do they want?** (What's the goal — stated or unstated?)
3. **Why NOW?** (Why is this story happening at this moment?)

If you can't answer all three, the audience won't know why they should care.

## Foundation 2: The Stickiness Checklist

> Based on *Made to Stick* (Chip & Dan Heath)

A sticky idea is one people remember, retell, and act on. The SUCCESs framework provides six dimensions. Use this checklist in Stage 4 (Judge) to evaluate any story.

### The SUCCESs Checklist

| Letter | Principle | Question to Ask | Weak → Strong Example |
|:-------|:----------|:----------------|:----------------------|
| **S** | **Simple** | Can I state the core idea in one sentence? | "Our company makes software" → "We help doctors spot strokes 3x faster." |
| **U** | **Unexpected** | Does the first 10 seconds surprise the audience? | "Here are our quarterly results" → "I'm going to tell you why our best quarter was also our worst." |
| **C** | **Concrete** | Can the audience picture what I'm describing? | "Improved efficiency metrics" → "Nurses now spend 22 more minutes per shift at the bedside." |
| **C** | **Credible** | Why should they believe me? | "Studies show..." → "Let me show you the chart from last Tuesday's shift." |
| **E** | **Emotional** | Do they care about the people in the story? | "Customer satisfaction rose 15%" → "Mrs Chen got her test results in 4 hours instead of 3 days." |
| **S** | **Story** | Does the narrative itself carry the message? | (If you're just listing facts, you're not storytelling.) |

### The "Curse of Knowledge" Buster

The Curse of Knowledge: once you know something, it's nearly impossible to imagine not knowing it. It's the #1 reason expert storytellers fail — they assume the audience knows what they know.

**Anti-Curse Exercise:** Before any story, the storyteller must write down:
1. "What does my audience already know about this topic?"
2. "What do I know that they DON'T know?"
3. "What's the MINIMUM they need to understand for the story to work?"

Cut everything that doesn't pass this test.

## Foundation 3: The Story Pipeline — How to Generate Material

> Based on *Storyworthy* (Matthew Dicks)

The #1 complaint from students: "I don't have any stories to tell." This pipeline solves that.

### Homework for Life

Spend 5 minutes at the end of each day writing one sentence about a moment that mattered — not the whole day, just **one moment**.

**The rule:** If you wouldn't stop a friend at a dinner party to tell them this moment, it's not the right moment.

After 30 days, a student has 30 potential story seeds. After a semester, over 100.

### The 5-Second Crystal Moment

Every great story contains a single moment of transformation that lasts 5 seconds or less. The entire story exists to deliver the audience to that moment.

**Exercise:** Ask students to scan their Homework for Life entries and find the one moment where they *changed* — where they saw something differently, made a decision, or felt something shift. That's the crystal moment. The story is built AROUND that moment.

### The "Dinner Test"

> Would this story hold the attention of a friend at a dinner table who doesn't know you?

If the answer is no, the story needs:
- More conflict (higher stakes)
- More specificity (fewer abstractions)
- More vulnerability (why should they care about YOU?)

## Quick Integration: Where These Fit in the 5-Stage Framework

| Foundation | Primary Stage | How to Use |
|:-----------|:--------------|:-----------|
| Prediction Error | Stage 1: Hook | Start with the unexpected. The brain needs to close the gap. |
| Sacred Flaw | Stage 2: Shape | Every character needs a wrong belief to overcome. |
| First Sentence 3 Questions | Stage 2: Shape | The minimum viable setup for any story. |
| Crystal Moment | Stage 2: Shape | Build the entire arc around the 5-second transformation. |
| SUCCESs Checklist | Stage 4: Judge | Final pass: is the story simple, unexpected, concrete, credible, emotional? |
| Curse of Knowledge Buster | Stage 4: Judge | Check for insider assumptions. |
| Dinner Test | Stage 4: Judge | The ultimate audience check. |
| Homework for Life | Pre-Stage (pipeline) | Ongoing practice. Not tied to a single story. |
