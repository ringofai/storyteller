# Stage 2: SHAPE — What's the Story's Spine?

## Craft Principle

**Give the hero a spine. Build 3-act structure. Find the Why.**

A hook gets attention. A shape keeps it. The shape of a story — its underlying structure — is what makes an audience stay engaged from beginning to end. Without it, you have moments of brilliance with no destination.

> "The most powerful stories have a single spine: a character who wants something desperately and must change to get it." — Andrew Stanton (Pixar)

> [!abstract] The Master's Decision — Why Three Acts Is Enough
> I've read Truby's 22-step system, Snyder's 15-beat sheet, and dozens of other structural models. They're all useful. But for 90% of the stories students need to tell — a 3-minute presentation, a 500-word essay, a 60-second pitch — the Three-Act Structure is sufficient and superior.
> 
> Why? More steps don't make better stories. They make students spend more time *thinking about structure* and less time *feeling the story*. The Three-Act Structure works because it maps to how humans naturally process narratives: before → during → after. Setup → conflict → resolution.
> 
> I add the SCRR structure (Setup-Challenge-Response-Resolution) as an alternative, not a replacement, because some students find four beats easier than three. But the Three Acts remain the default.
> 
> *What I've seen fail:* Students who use all 15 beats of *Save the Cat!* for a 3-minute story. The beats are designed for 90-minute films. Use the right tool for the right length.
> 
> *What I've seen succeed:* Students who can tell me their entire story in three sentences — one per act. If they can't, the structure isn't clear enough to build on.

## The Three-Act Structure

Every great story, from Aristotle's *Poetics* to the iPhone launch, follows the same shape:

```
  What Is (Act 1)  ───→  The Gap (Act 2)  ───→  New Bliss (Act 3)
       │                      │                        │
       │                      │                        │
       ▼                      ▼                        ▼
  Status quo            Tension & conflict         Resolution
  established           Inciting incident          Call to action
```

### Act 1: Exposition (What Is)

Establish the status quo. Show the audience where things are now.

- Who is the hero? What do they want?
- What's the world like before the story happens?
- What's at stake if nothing changes?

### Act 2: Rising Action (The Gap)

Create tension by comparing "what is" with "what could be." Make the gap as big as possible. This is the inciting incident — the audience now *has* to decide whether to join you.

- What obstacle appears?
- What does the hero try and fail?
- What's the moment of greatest doubt?

### Act 3: Resolution (New Bliss)

Move through resistance to describe the world *after* the idea is adopted.

- How does the hero change?
- What does the new world look like?
- What's the single action the audience should take?

## Exercise: Build Your Story Spine

Write three paragraphs — one per act — answering:

**Act 1:** "Once upon a time, [HERO] lived in a world where [STATUS QUO]. Every day, [ROUTINE]. But one thing was missing: [WHAT THE HERO WANTS]."

**Act 2:** "Then one day, [INCITING INCIDENT]. At first, the hero tried [FAILED ATTEMPT]. The situation got worse: [LOWEST POINT]. But hidden inside the problem was [THE TRUTH]."

**Act 3:** "Finally, the hero realised [KEY INSIGHT]. The world changed: [NEW BLISS]. And that's why [CALL TO ACTION]."

## Character Spine + Sacred Flaw

Every character in a story needs a **spine** — a dominant, often unconscious goal that drives everything they do.

- Woody's spine (Toy Story): Do what's best for his child
- WALL-E's spine: Find beauty
- Marlin's spine (Finding Nemo): Keep his son safe

**Exercise:** In one sentence, what does your hero want MORE than anything? If they got it, would the story end? If they lost it, would the story end? You've found the spine.

### The "Sacred Flaw"

> From *The Science of Storytelling* (Will Storr)

The most compelling protagonists are not perfect — they have a **deeply held but wrong belief**. The story's job is to challenge, complicate, or shatter that belief.

**Exercise:** "What does your protagonist believe about themselves that isn't true?" If the answer is "nothing," the character isn't ready.

| Story | Protagonist | Sacred Flaw | What Shatters It |
|:------|:------------|:-------------|:-----------------|
| Finding Nemo | Marlin | "The ocean is dangerous; safety is everything." | Nemo's courage forces him to trust. |
| Toy Story | Woody | "My value comes from being Andy's favourite." | Buzz shows him belonging isn't rank. |

### Character Interrogation — Don't Assume, Ask

> From Mark W. Travis / Film Courage

The #1 mistake in character creation: the writer *assumes* they know the character. Instead, **ask the character questions** and discover them.

**Classroom exercise:** Pair students. Student A writes a one-paragraph character sketch. Student B plays interrogator — ask curious questions ("What are you most afraid of?"), tough questions ("Who have you hurt?"), and supportive questions ("Who do you trust?"). Student A must answer *as the character*. The answers reveal the real spine.

### "Save the Cat" Moment

> From *Save the Cat!* (Blake Snyder)

The audience decides whether to root for a protagonist in the first 10 minutes. The hero must do something likeable early — save a cat, help a stranger, show kindness without reward.

**Classroom check:** "What has the hero done by page 1 / minute 1 to make the audience like them?" If nothing, add a beat before the inciting incident.

## The SCRR Structure (Alternative to Three-Act)

> From Philipp Humm / StoryLab

For simpler stories, use a four-sentence structure instead of three acts:

| Step | Meaning | One-Sentence Template |
|:-----|:--------|:----------------------|
| **S** — Setup | Where are we? | "It was a Tuesday afternoon and I was presenting to the board." |
| **C** — Challenge | What went wrong? | "The projector died 30 seconds in." |
| **R** — Response | What did you do? | "I threw the slides away and told the story from memory." |
| **R** — Resolution | What happened? | "The CEO said it was the best presentation she'd seen all year." |

**Classroom exercise:** Give students 5 minutes to write a 60-second story using exactly this structure. Enforce the 4-sentence maximum. The constraint forces precision.

### The "Who's the Hero?" Check

> From *Building a StoryBrand* (Donald Miller)

The storyteller is NOT the hero. **The audience is.** You are the guide (Yoda, not Luke).

**Three-Level Problem** — Every hero faces three layers:

| Level | Question | Example |
|:------|:---------|:--------|
| **External** | What's the concrete obstacle? | "I have to give a speech in front of 500 people." |
| **Internal** | What's the emotional frustration? | "I'm terrified of being judged by strangers." |
| **Philosophical** | Why does this matter beyond me? | "If I can't speak in public, I'll never advocate for causes I believe in." |

**Exercise:** After students outline their story, map all three problem levels. Most only identify the external — the internal and philosophical layers are where emotional connection lives.

## Bilingual Adaptation

- **Linear vs. circular:** English stories tend to follow a direct A→B→C arc. Cantonese and Mandarin narratives may use a circular structure — returning to the beginning at the end for resonance.
- **The "Why" in collectivist contexts:** Sinek's "Start with Why" in an individualist framework often means "my purpose." In collectivist cultures, the "Why" may need to be framed as "our purpose" or "my community's purpose."
- Let your audience's cultural expectations guide how tightly you structure the arc — some cultures leave more room for ambiguity.

## Play-Based Warm-Up (LSP-Inspired)

**"The Three-Brick Story"** — Give each person three LEGO bricks. Ask them to build three models: one representing "What Is," one representing "The Gap," and one representing "New Bliss." Then tell the story that connects them. This makes abstract structure physically visible.

## Bravery Exercise — Write the Scene You're Avoiding

Every story has a moment the writer wants to skip — the scene that's too painful, too awkward, or too revealing. That scene is almost always the most important one.

**Task:** Ask yourself: "What's the one scene in this story I most want to skip?" Write that scene. In detail. No censoring.

**Why this works:** The desire to skip is a diagnostic. It means there's something true here, and the student's protective instinct is trying to avoid it. Writing the avoided scene forces the protective instinct to step aside.

**Classroom note:** Students who do this exercise often produce their best writing in under 10 minutes. The constraint is emotional, not technical — and emotion is a faster engine than technique.
