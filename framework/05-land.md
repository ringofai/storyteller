# Stage 5: LAND — How Do I Make It Stick?

## Craft Principle

**Deliver with presence. Use voice, silence, and conversation skills.**

A great story poorly delivered is a great story nobody remembers. The final stage is about bridging the gap between the story on the page and the story in the room — whether that room is physical, virtual, or one-to-one.

> "Most of us don't listen with the intent to understand. We listen with the intent to reply." — Celeste Headlee

> [!abstract] The Master's Decision — Why Land Is a Stage, Not an Afterthought
> Most storytelling frameworks end at "write your story." Delivery is treated as optional — something you figure out on the day.
> 
> I made Land a full stage because I've seen too many good stories fail at the moment of truth. A student with a brilliant story who mumbles, reads from notes, or rushes through the ending will be remembered as "nervous" — not "brilliant."
> 
> Julian Treasure's HAIL framework made the cut because it's the most teachable delivery system I've found. It gives students something to practise (vocal exercises take 90 seconds) rather than vague advice ("be confident").
> 
> I added the 3-Phase Emotional Journey from Charisma on Command because students who nail their structure still often deliver it flat. The emotional arc is the difference between a story that's understood and a story that's felt.
> 
> *What I've seen fail:* Students who memorise their entire script and panic when they forget a line. The 3-point structure (Alexander Lyon) solution is deliberately minimal — three points, not a script.
> 
> *What I've seen succeed:* The 90-second vocal warm-up. Students who do this before presenting are visibly more confident. It's the single highest-ROI exercise in the entire framework.

## The Vocal Toolbox

Every speaker has six tools. Most people use only two or three. Great storytellers use all six deliberately.

| Tool | What It Does | How to Practise |
|:-----|:-------------|:----------------|
| **Register** | Chest voice = weight and authority. Nasal = weak. | Place your hand on your chest. Speak. Feel the vibration. That's your power register. |
| **Timbre** | Warm, rich, smooth sound is preferred and trainable. | Hum until you feel it in your lips. That buzz is your optimal timbre. |
| **Prosody** | The sing-song meta-language. Monotone loses audiences. | Read a sentence three ways: as a question, a command, and a confession. |
| **Pace** | Speed up for excitement. Slow down for emphasis. | Read a paragraph. Then read it at half speed. Mark words that need slowing. |
| **Pitch** | Higher pitch = arousal or question. Lower = statement or certainty. | End statements with a downward inflection. End questions with upward. |
| **Volume** | Get quiet to command attention. Loud = "sodcasting" (rude). | Practise a sentence at three volumes: room, close conversation, whisper. |

## The Seven Deadly Sins of Speaking

Avoid these (from Julian Treasure's research):

1. **Gossip** — Speaking ill of someone who's not present
2. **Judging** — Making the audience feel evaluated
3. **Negativity** — You can be honest without being negative
4. **Complaining** — Viral misery without productive purpose
5. **Excuses** — Defensiveness before criticism
6. **Exaggeration** — Lies that destroy trust when discovered
7. **Dogmatism** — The confusion of fact with opinion

## The 10 Rules of Great Conversation

From Celeste Headlee's research at NPR:

1. **Don't multitask** — Be present. If you can't be present, don't have the conversation.
2. **Don't pontificate** — Enter every conversation assuming you have something to learn.
3. **Use open-ended questions** — Who, what, when, where, why, how. Not yes/no.
4. **Go with the flow** — Let thoughts come and go. Don't stop listening to prepare your response.
5. **If you don't know, say so.** Talk should not be cheap.
6. **Don't equate their experience with yours.** It's not about you.
7. **Don't repeat yourself.** It's condescending and boring.
8. **Stay out of the weeds.** People don't care about the details; they care about what you're like.
9. **Listen.** (The most important.) "If your mouth is open, you're not learning."
10. **Be brief.** A good conversation is like a miniskirt — short enough to hold interest, long enough to cover the subject.

## Exercise: The First 60 Seconds

Record yourself delivering the first 60 seconds of your story. Then review:

- **Clarity:** Can you understand every word?
- **Energy:** Does your voice match the emotional tone?
- **Pacing:** Are there natural pauses?
- **Eye contact:** (If on video) Are you reading or connecting?
- **The 1-Sentence Test:** If someone walked in at second 30, would they know what the story is about?

### The Three-Phase Emotional Journey (Charisma on Command)

Instead of telling events in chronological order, guide the audience through an emotional arc:

| Phase | What Happens | Example |
|:------|:-------------|:--------|
| **Tension** | Create uncertainty — will they succeed? | "I had 30 seconds left and my mind was completely blank." |
| **Release** | The turning point — what happened next? | "Then I remembered the one thing my mentor told me." |
| **Insight** | Why this mattered — the lesson | "I learned that preparation isn't about having the right words. It's about trusting yourself to find them." |

**Classroom exercise:** Students write a 90-second story structured as Tension → Release → Insight. No more than 3 sentences per phase. The constraint forces them to identify the emotional high point and build toward it.

### The 3-Point Speech Structure (Alexander Lyon)

For any short presentation (2-5 min), use exactly three points:

```
Point 1 (2-3 sentences)
Point 2 (2-3 sentences)
Point 3 (2-3 sentences)
Conclusion: Restate the single idea + call to action
```

**Classroom exercise:** Students draw a random topic. They have 3 minutes to prepare a 1-minute speech using exactly 3 points. No notes. No slides. The constraint of "3 points" makes structure automatic.

## Vocal Warm-Up (HAIL Exercises)

Do these before any important delivery:

1. **Arms up + sigh** — Reach to the sky, deep breath, sigh out. Releases tension.
2. **Ba-Ba-Ba** — Lips together: "ba-ba-ba-ba-ba." Wakes up lip articulation.
3. **Brrrrr** — Lip trill. Like a horse. Loosens the lips.
4. **La-La-La** — Tongue to palate: "la-la-la-la-la." Wakes up tongue placement.
5. **Rrrrrr** — Rolled R. "Champagne for the tongue."
6. **The Siren** — "Weeeeee-aaaaaawwwww." Full vocal range warm-up.

Total time: 90 seconds.

## Bilingual Adaptation

- **Silence reads differently.** In English presentations, a 2-second pause signals confidence. In Cantonese and Mandarin settings, a prolonged pause may signal uncertainty or a gap in preparation. Know your audience's tempo.
- **Eye contact norms vary.** Direct, sustained eye contact is expected in Western presentation contexts. In East Asian settings, it may be perceived as confrontation. Adapt your delivery to the room.
- **Vocal variety** is universally effective, but what counts as "too much" varies. Test your delivery with a native speaker of each language.

## Play-Based Warm-Up (LSP-Inspired)

**"The Silence Build"** — Give participants 2 minutes to build something with LEGO bricks in COMPLETE silence. Then ask them to describe their build to a partner in 60 seconds, using only the vocal tools they just practised. The constraint of silence first makes them think non-verbally; the constraint of 60 seconds makes them choose every word carefully.

## Bravery Exercise — Deliver to a Stone Face

Students get comfortable with friendly audiences. The real world has board members, sceptics, and distracted listeners.

**Task:** One student delivers their story. One listener is instructed to give NO reaction — stone face, no nodding, no smiling, no eye contact. The storyteller must continue as if the listener is engaged.

Then switch roles.

**Why this works:** Students discover how much their delivery depends on audience feedback (nods, smiles, laughter). Without it, they speed up, lose confidence, or abandon their structure. The exercise trains them to hold the story's energy independently. It also reveals whether the story has intrinsic momentum or relies on the audience's permission to keep going.

**Classroom note:** After the exercise, ask the stone-faced listener: "What did you actually think of the story?" The contrast between the storyteller's anxiety and the listener's actual response is always illuminating.
