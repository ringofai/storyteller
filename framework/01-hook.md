# Stage 1: HOOK — Why Should Anyone Care?

## Craft Principle

**Start from the ending. Create a gap between "what is" and "what could be."**

The first 60 seconds of any story determine whether your audience leans in or zones out. Nancy Duarte's analysis of the world's greatest talks (MLK, Steve Jobs, Churchill) reveals a consistent pattern: the speaker alternates between describing "what is" (the current reality) and "what could be" (a possible future). The gap between them creates **tension** — and tension is what makes people listen.

> "The first 60 seconds determine everything. Start with the outcome, not the process." — Nancy Duarte

> [!abstract] The Master's Decision — Why Hook Comes First
> Most storytelling frameworks start with structure (Three Acts, Hero's Journey). I deliberately start with Hook because of a pattern I've seen in dozens of workshops: when students build a structure first, they produce a perfectly organised story that nobody wants to hear. The structure is sound, but the audience has no reason to lean in.
> 
> Hook-first means students must answer "Why should anyone care?" before they invest time in structure. If they can't find a hook, they shouldn't be building an outline yet. This saves hours of wasted effort.
> 
> *What I've seen fail:* Starting with "Once upon a time..." Students default to chronological, safe openings. The four-hook-types exercise (below) exists specifically to break this habit.
> 
> *What I've seen succeed:* Students who write their ending first — literally the last sentence — and then work backwards. The ending is the destination. Everything else is the journey to get there.

## Three Hook Types

| Type | How It Works | Example |
|:-----|:-------------|:--------|
| **The Promise Hook** | Make a promise in the opening that propels the audience forward | "By the end of this talk, you'll see your kitchen differently." |
| **The Gap Hook** | Reveal a disconnection in the audience's worldview | "Everything you know about motivation is wrong." |
| **The Wonder Hook** | Invoke curiosity by withholding one piece of information | "I'm going to tell you why a 4-year-old solved a problem 500 engineers couldn't." |

### Moxie Institute's Four Hook Types (Classroom Exercise)

From the Moxie Institute's IMPACT framework, there are four distinct ways to open a story. Teach students to deliberately choose, not default:

| Type | Description | Example |
|:-----|:------------|:--------|
| **In Medias Res** | Start in the middle of action | "The car was already rolling when I jumped." |
| **Counter-Intuitive** | Start with a contradiction | "I should have felt great. Instead, I wanted to cry." |
| **Sensory Scene** | Start with a vivid sensory detail | "The smell of burnt toast was the first thing I noticed." |
| **Direct Address** | Start by speaking to the audience | "Have you ever had a moment where everything changed in five seconds?" |

**Classroom exercise:** Prepare 4 cards, each with one hook type. Students draw a card and write two opening sentences for a given topic (e.g. "a time I failed") using that hook type. Share and discuss which hooks landed and why. This trains students to **design** their opening, not default to "once upon a time."

## Exercise: Write Your Ending First

**Step 1:** In one sentence, write what you want your audience to remember, feel, or do after your story ends.

**Step 2:** In one sentence, write where your audience is *right now* — their current belief, behaviour, or situation.

**Step 3:** The gap between Step 2 and Step 1 is your hook. Write it in one sentence.

**Step 4:** Test your hook: "If someone heard ONLY this hook, would they want to hear more?"

## Bilingual Adaptation

- **English hooks** tend to be direct, explicit, and benefit-oriented: "You'll learn how to X."
- **Cantonese hooks** can be more evocative and relational, appealing to shared understanding.
- **Mandarin hooks** may benefit from analogy or philosophical framing.
- When adapting a hook across languages, **don't translate the words — re-craft the promise.**

## Play-Based Warm-Up (LSP-Inspired)

**"The Object Hook"** — Bring any small object to the group. Give them 60 seconds to invent a story hook inspired by that object. The object has no inherent meaning — they must CREATE the hook from zero. This exercise reveals that hooks come from constraint, not inspiration.

## Bravery Exercise — Show Your Worst Opening

Most students spend time polishing their opening until it's "good enough." This exercise reverses that.

**Task:** Write the worst possible opening for your story. Make it clichéd, boring, confusing, or embarrassing. Then share it with a partner.

**Why this works:** After writing the worst opening, students immediately know what NOT to do — and paradoxically, they also see what they WANT to do. The bad version clarifies the good version. More importantly, it lowers the stakes: if the worst version is shared and nobody died, the student can now take a real risk with the real version.

**Classroom note:** The facilitator should go first. Show your own worst opening. Laugh at yourself. This gives students permission to be bad in public, which is the prerequisite for being good in public.
