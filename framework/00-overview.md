# The Storyteller Framework — Overview

## Core Principle

Stories are not born — they are **built**. The Storyteller Framework is a 5-stage system that takes any story idea from first spark to final delivery. Each stage has a clear craft principle, a practical exercise, an AI prompt, and a measurable output.

---

## The Editor's Note: Why These Frameworks, Why This Order

Every framework in this system survived a simple test: *"Does this help a student who has 45 minutes to prepare a story they care about?"*

But there's a deeper reason I'm curating rather than creating. The new generation has access to more creative tools than any before — AI that writes, generates, and produces at the push of a button. What they have less of is **practice in creative appreciation and judgement**: the ability to look at a story and know what's working, what's not, and why.

That's the muscle this framework exists to rebuild. Every exercise, every checklist, every rubric is designed not just to help students *produce* a story, but to help them *judge* one — their own and others'. The frameworks I've chosen are the ones that develop that critical eye most efficiently.

Here's what didn't make the cut:
- **The Hero's Journey** (Campbell) — brilliant for analysing *The Odyssey*, confusing for a 16-year-old writing about their summer internship. It's a map of world mythology, not a practical tool.
- **The 22-Step Structure** (Truby) — too many beats. Students got lost in the steps and forgot the story. I kept only the Character Web and the concept of organic structure.
- **The Moth storytelling rules** — excellent for live storytelling, but they assume a 10-minute spoken format. This framework covers written, video, and presentation formats too.

**Why this order:** Most storytelling courses start with structure (Three Acts, Hero's Journey). Students dutifully outline their story — and then lose interest because they don't yet know *why* anyone should care. That's why this framework starts with **Hook**, not **Shape**. You can't build a structure until you know what gap you're trying to create.

**What I've learned teaching this:** The stages are not strictly linear. In practice, students jump back and forth. A student who reaches LAND and bombs their delivery often realises their HOOK was wrong. A student in FLESH who can't find a metaphor often discovers their SHAPE was missing a character spine. The stages are a **diagnostic tool** — if one stage is painful, it's telling you something about the stage before.

## The Five Stages

```
  ┌─────────┐    ┌─────────┐    ┌─────────┐    ┌─────────┐    ┌─────────┐
  │  HOOK   │ →  │  SHAPE  │ →  │  FLESH  │ →  │  JUDGE  │ →  │  LAND   │
  │         │    │         │    │         │    │         │    │         │
  │ Grab    │    │ Build   │    │ Write & │    │ Test &  │    │ Deliver │
  │ attention│   │ structure│   │ produce │    │ refine  │    │ & impact│
  └─────────┘    └─────────┘    └─────────┘    └─────────┘    └─────────┘
```

| Stage | Core Question | Craft Principle | Key Output | Time |
|:------|:--------------|:----------------|:-----------|:-----|
| **1. HOOK** | Why should anyone care? | Start from the ending. Create a gap between "what is" and "what could be." | One-line hook + audience map | 15-30 min |
| **2. SHAPE** | What's the story's spine? | Give the hero a spine. Build 3-act structure. Find the Why. | Story outline + character spine | 30-45 min |
| **3. FLESH** | How do I bring it to life? | Show, don't tell. Use metaphor, data, and multi-modal production. | Script + storyboard + draft assets | 60-120 min |
| **4. JUDGE** | Does it work? | Test for clarity, emotion, ethics, and audience understanding. | Feedback notes + revision targets | 20-40 min |
| **5. LAND** | How do I make it stick? | Deliver with presence. Use voice, silence, and conversation skills. | Final presentation + reflection | 30-60 min |

## 10 Founding Frameworks (Why These 10?)

The Storyteller Framework synthesises principles from 10 storytelling frameworks and adapts them for the AI age. Each one was chosen because it passed the classroom test — not just the library test.

1. **Aristotle's Poetics** — *Why:* 3,000 years old and still the best foundation. *Why here:* Provides the universal arc that all students recognise, even if they can't name it.

2. **Nancy Duarte's "Resonance"** — *Why:* The only framework that explains *how* to create tension (What Is vs. What Could Be), not just *that* stories need tension.

3. **Andrew Stanton (Pixar)** — *Why:* Six specific, memorable clues that students actually remember. *Adaptation:* Stanton wrote for 90-minute films. I've compressed his clues into exercises that work in 10 minutes.

4. **Chris Anderson (TED)** — *Why:* His "one idea" rule is the single most important editing tool for students who want to cram everything into one story.

5. **Simon Sinek** — *Why:* The Golden Circle works because it's simple and counters the instinct to start with facts. *Limitation:* Needs cultural adaptation for non-Western audiences (see Bilingual section).

6. **Julian Treasure** — *Why:* Most storytelling frameworks ignore delivery entirely. Treasure gives students something they can practise in 90 seconds.

7. **Celeste Headlee** — *Why:* Storytelling isn't just speaking — it's listening. Her rules are the best framework for the conversational dimension.

8. **Brené Brown** — *Why:* Without vulnerability, stories are content, not connection. *What I've learned:* Students resist vulnerability at first. The exercises are designed to build up to it gradually.

9. **Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic** — *Why:* The best framework for data storytelling — which most narrative frameworks simply ignore.

10. **Nancy Duarte's 60-Second Hook** — *Why:* A standalone technique that solves the single hardest part of storytelling (the opening) with a repeatable method.

## What the Framework Adds Beyond the Sources

These sources were the raw material. Here's what I built on top:

- **AI integration** at every stage — specific prompts tested in workshops, not generic "use ChatGPT" advice
- **Production pipeline** — from storyboard to video/audio assets. Most frameworks stop at writing.
- **Assessment rubrics** — because educators told me "I love this, but how do I grade it?"
- **Bilingual narrative logic** — English favours linear, explicit resolution. Cantonese and Mandarin may favour circular, implicit resonance. I've navigated both.
- **Play-based warm-ups** — LSP-inspired exercises that bypass the "I don't know what to write" freeze
- **Train-the-trainer layer** — workshop blueprints for educators who want to teach this to others

## Quick Reference: The 60-Minute Story

1. **Hook (10 min)** — Write your ending first. Find the gap.
2. **Shape (15 min)** — Three acts. One character spine.
3. **Flesh (20 min)** — Write 250 words. One metaphor. One sensory detail.
4. **Judge (5 min)** — Read aloud. Ask the single-idea test.
5. **Land (10 min)** — First 60 seconds. Chest voice. One pause. CTA.

---

## Language Note

This framework was developed bilingually (English, Cantonese, Mandarin) for workshops in Hong Kong. Story structures that work in English (linear, low-context, explicit resolution) may need adaptation for Cantonese and Mandarin contexts (circular, high-context, implicit resonance). Where relevant, each stage file includes bilingual adaptation notes. These aren't theoretical — they come from observing what actually worked and didn't in trilingual classrooms.
