# Stage 3: FLESH — How Do I Bring It to Life?

## Craft Principle

**Show, don't tell. Use metaphor, concrete detail, and multi-modal production.**

Structure gives a story its skeleton. Flesh gives it life. This is where the outline becomes a script, the script becomes a storyboard, and the storyboard becomes a finished piece — whether text, audio, video, or slides.

> "A well-organised absence of information draws us in. Give the audience two plus two, not four." — Andrew Stanton (Pixar)

> [!abstract] The Master's Decision — Why Production Belongs in the Framework
> Most storytelling frameworks stop at writing. They assume the story ends when the last word is written. But in the real world — advertising, education, film — the story doesn't end until it's *produced and delivered*.
> 
> That's why Stage 3 includes storyboarding, multi-modal production, and the sensory substitution exercise. A story that exists only as text hasn't been tested. A student who writes a script but never storyboards it will miss pacing problems that only become visible in visuals.
> 
> *What I've seen fail:* Students who spend all their time perfecting the written script and have nothing left for production. The 250-Word Rule exists specifically to prevent this — force the essence first, then expand.
> 
> *What I've seen succeed:* Students who produce a rough storyboard (6 frames, stick figures) before writing a single line of script. The visual constraints reveal structural problems that words can hide.

## The Fleshing-Out Toolbox

| Tool | What It Does | Example |
|:-----|:-------------|:--------|
| **Metaphor** | Makes the unfamiliar familiar by connecting to what they already know | "CRISPR is a word processor for DNA." |
| **Sensory Detail** | Makes the abstract physical — engage the five senses | Not "she was nervous" but "her fingers tapped the table in morse code." |
| **The "Two Plus Two" Rule** | Let the audience complete the pattern themselves | Show the setup and the result; let them infer the middle. |
| **Concrete Over Abstract** | Replace general nouns with specific ones | Not "an animal" but "a three-legged dog named Lucky." |
| **The Moment of Vulnerability** | Show imperfection to create connection | Not "I succeeded" but "I failed three times before I understood." |

## Exercise: From Outline to Script

Take your three-act outline and expand each act:

**Act 1:** Start with the hook you wrote in Stage 1. Add one sensory detail. Show the status quo through a specific moment, not a general statement.

**Act 2:** Add the moment of greatest tension. Use the "Two Plus Two" rule — give the audience half the information and let them complete the pattern.

**Act 3:** End with the call to action from your original hook. One concrete image of the "New Bliss."

**The 250-Word Rule:** Write the full story in 250 words first. If you can't tell it in 250, you haven't found the essence yet.

### Sensory Substitution Exercise

> From Quotidian Writer (Diane Callahan)

**The problem:** Students write "he was nervous" or "she felt sad" — they *tell* instead of *show*.

**The fix:** Replace every emotional abstraction with a sensory detail from at least two senses.

| Telling (weak) | Showing (strong) |
|:---------------|:-----------------|
| "He was nervous." | "His fingers tapped the table in morse code. The air in the room felt too thin to breathe." |
| "She was excited." | "She bounced on her heels and laughed at nothing. The afternoon light seemed brighter than it had any right to be." |
| "They were confused." | "They looked at each other. Then back at the map. Then at each other again." |

**Classroom exercise:** Give students 10 "telling" sentences. They rewrite each using at least two sensory details. Time limit: 10 minutes. Output: 10 rewritten sentences. This trains the brain to default to sensory language.

## AI Prompt Templates

**For fleshing out the script:**
```
Here is my three-act outline: [OUTLINE]

Help me expand each act into a full narrative. For each act, suggest:
1) A metaphor that makes the central idea concrete
2) One sensory detail (what does it look, sound, feel, smell, or taste like?)
3) A moment where the hero shows vulnerability
4) A "two plus two" moment where the audience fills in the gap
```

**For storyboarding:**
```
Here is my script: [SCRIPT]

Help me create a visual storyboard with 6-8 key scenes. For each scene:
1) Describe the visual frame
2) Describe the audio (dialogue, music, silence)
3) State the emotional tone
4) Suggest the transition to the next frame
```

**For data-driven stories:**
```
Here is my story idea about [TOPIC]. I have these data points: [DATA].

Help me weave the data into a narrative. What's the best visual format for each data point? What's the "human story" behind the numbers? How do I transition between data points without losing narrative momentum?
```

"If someone consumed only the output — read, watched, or listened — would they feel the story's full emotional arc?"

## Bilingual Adaptation

- **Metaphors don't translate.** A metaphor that works in English (e.g. "the elephant in the room") may be meaningless in Cantonese or Mandarin. Re-craft metaphors for each language context.
- **Sensory detail is universal.** A specific smell, texture, or sound works across languages — prioritise sensory detail over wordplay when crossing cultures.
- **Vulnerability reads differently.** What counts as "brave honesty" in one culture may be "oversharing" in another. Know your audience's comfort zone.

## Play-Based Warm-Up (LSP-Inspired)

**"Build the Emotion"** — Ask participants to build a model of a single emotion from their story using LEGO bricks (anger, hope, fear, joy — whatever fits). Then ask them to describe what they built. The physical act of building an abstract feeling unlocks sensory language they wouldn't find through writing alone.

## Bravery Exercise — Write the Same Scene Three Ways

Most students write the first version that comes to mind and stop. The first version is almost never the best version.

**Task:** Take one scene from your story. Write it three different ways:
1. The version that's most honest (even if it's uncomfortable)
2. The version that's most dramatic (even if it's exaggerated)
3. The version that's most surprising (even if it changes the story)

**Why this works:** Students discover that the first version is usually the safest, not the best. Constraint (three versions, three different angles) forces them past their default. Often, the best material comes from version 3 — which they never would have found if they'd stopped at version 1.

**Classroom note:** Give students 5 minutes per version. The time pressure prevents overthinking. Share only version 3.
