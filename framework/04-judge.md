# Stage 4: JUDGE — Does It Work?

## Craft Principle

**Test for clarity, emotion, ethics, and audience understanding.**

Before a story goes public, it needs to survive its own scrutiny. This stage is not about perfection — it's about ensuring the story does what you intend it to do. A story that works for you may not work for your audience. A story that works today may not work tomorrow.

But there's a deeper purpose to this stage. Of all five stages, Judge is where **creative appreciation and judgement** is built. Anyone can write a story. Not everyone can look at a story — their own or someone else's — and articulate what's working, what's not, and why. That skill is becoming rarer in an age of instant AI generation, and it's the skill this stage exists to develop.

> "If it doesn't end with a decision, you've made a chart, not a story." — Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic

> [!abstract] The Master's Decision — Why Judge Comes Before Land
> The instinct is to go straight from writing to presenting. Most students want to perform their story immediately — they're proud of what they wrote and eager to show it.
> 
> That's exactly why Judge must come before Land. I've seen too many students deliver a story that had a brilliant hook and a beautiful structure — but the audience was confused because the single idea wasn't clear. A 2-minute fix in the writing stage would have saved a 10-minute uncomfortable presentation.
> 
> The SUCCESs checklist, Dinner Test, and Previous Page Test are deliberately placed here, not earlier. If students used them in Flesh, they'd over-optimise too early. If they used them in Land, it would be too late. Judge is the moment of cold evaluation before the heat of delivery.
> 
> *What I've seen fail:* Students who skip the ethics check because "it's just a personal story." The most dangerous stories are the ones the teller believes are harmless.
> 
> *What I've seen succeed:* The "Three Loves / One Suggestion" feedback structure. Students are more honest with each other than they'd ever be with a teacher — and they accept peer feedback more readily.

## The Four Self-Checks

### 1. The Single-Idea Test
**Question:** Is there ONE clear idea running through the entire story?
**How to check:** If you had to explain this story in a single sentence, could you? Would two people agree on what that sentence is?

### 2. The Worth-Sharing Test
**Question:** Does this story benefit someone other than me?
**How to check:** Chris Anderson's test — "Who does this serve?" If it only serves the storyteller, the audience will see through it.

### 3. The Vulnerability Authenticity Test
**Question:** Is the vulnerability authentic, not performative?
**How to check:** Brené Brown's insight — "You cannot selectively numb emotion." If the vulnerable moment is included purely for effect, the audience will feel manipulated.

### 4. The AI Ethics Test
**Question:** Has AI been used to amplify, not replace, the human voice?
**How to check:** Read the story aloud. Does it still sound like you? If you swapped in a different AI prompt and got a different story, the human voice is missing.

## The SUCCESs Stickiness Checklist

> From *Made to Stick* (Chip & Dan Heath)

Before a story goes public, check it against six dimensions of memorability:

| ✓ | Principle | Question | Your Score (1-5) |
|:-:|:----------|:---------|:-----------------|
| ☐ | **Simple** | Can I state the core idea in one sentence? | |
| ☐ | **Unexpected** | Does the first 10 seconds surprise the audience? | |
| ☐ | **Concrete** | Can the audience picture what I'm describing? | |
| ☐ | **Credible** | Why should they believe me? | |
| ☐ | **Emotional** | Do they care about the people in the story? | |
| ☐ | **Story** | Does the narrative carry the message? (Not list of facts) | |

**Target:** All six at 4 or above. Any score ≤ 3 needs revision.

### The Curse of Knowledge Buster

Once you know something, it's nearly impossible to imagine not knowing it. Before any story:

1. What does my audience already know about this topic?
2. What do I know that they DON'T know?
3. What's the MINIMUM they need for the story to work?

Cut everything that doesn't pass this test.

## The Three Attention Tests

### The Dinner Test

> From *Storyworthy* (Matthew Dicks)

Would this story hold the attention of a stranger at a dinner table? If no, the story needs:
- More **conflict** (higher stakes)
- More **specificity** (fewer abstractions)
- More **vulnerability** (why should they care?)

### The Previous Page Test

> From *Wired for Story* (Lisa Cron)

For each section of the story, ask: "If the audience only experienced the material BEFORE this section, would they NEED to know what comes next?" If the answer is no, cut it. The brain tracks cause-and-effect chains — every beat must create an unanswered question.

### The Scene Three-Point Check

> From Bookfox (John Fox)

Every scene must pass three checks:

1. **Does it advance the plot?** (Does something change?)
2. **Does the character change?** (Do they learn, decide, or shift?)
3. **Is there conflict?** (Is something at stake?)

If any answer is "no," the scene needs revision or removal.

## Peer Feedback Exercise

Pair up and use this feedback structure:

**Three Loves:**
1. _________________________________
2. _________________________________
3. _________________________________

**One Suggestion:**
1. _________________________________

**One Question:**
1. What part of the story do you want to know more about?

> Rule: The storyteller stays silent during feedback. Listen fully before responding.

## Assessment Rubrics

### For Secondary Students (13-17)

| Criterion | Emerging (1) | Developing (2) | Proficient (3) | Advanced (4) |
|:----------|:-------------|:---------------|:---------------|:-------------|
| **Hook** | Has a beginning | Opening creates curiosity | Opening creates a clear gap between "what is" and "what could be" | Opening is memorable, specific, and emotionally resonant |
| **Structure** | Has a beginning and an end | Has beginning, middle, end | Three-act structure is visible with clear transitions | Structure is purposeful and serves the emotional arc |
| **Character** | A person appears | The hero wants something | The hero has a clear spine and faces an obstacle | The hero changes through the story |
| **Delivery** | Story is told | Eye contact and pacing are attempted | Voice varies (pace, pitch, volume) | Presence is confident; silence is used effectively |

### For University Students (18-22)

| Criterion | Developing (1) | Competent (2) | Strong (3) | Excellent (4) |
|:----------|:---------------|:--------------|:-----------|:--------------|
| **Hook** | Gets attention | Creates a clear gap | Hook type is intentional and matched to audience | Hook is surprising, memorable, and sets up the entire arc |
| **Structure** | Has arc | Three-act structure is clear | Acts are balanced; transitions are smooth | Structure is invisible to the audience but purposeful to the storyteller |
| **Fleshing Out** | Script is complete | Uses metaphor or sensory detail | Multi-modal (e.g. storyboard + script) | Production choices serve the story's emotional goal |
| **Vulnerability** | Personal element included | Vulnerability feels earned | Vulnerability is specific and contextual | Vulnerability is the engine of connection |
| **Landing** | Ends with a point | Ends with a call to action | Delivery is rehearsed and natural | The audience remembers one sentence; the rest supports it |
| **AI Use** | AI was used | AI prompts are documented | AI amplified, not replaced, the voice | Story would be weaker without AI, but unmistakably human |

## Bilingual Adaptation

- **The Single-Idea Test in translation:** When moving between languages, check that the single idea survives. An idea that's crystal clear in English may fragment in Cantonese or Mandarin. Test the story in each language version independently.
- **The Worth-Sharing Test in different cultures:** What counts as "worth sharing" varies. A story about individual triumph may be more valued in Western contexts; a story about collective resilience may resonate more in East Asian contexts.

## Play-Based Warm-Up (LSP-Inspired)

**"The Blind Critique"** — One person tells their story. The listeners build a model with LEGO bricks representing the story's *emotional shape* (not its literal content). The storyteller then looks at what was built — and sees whether their intended emotion was received. This surfaces gaps between intention and impact faster than verbal feedback ever could.

## Bravery Exercise — Cut 50%

Most students' stories have 30-50% more content than they need. But cutting feels like losing.

**Task:** Cut your story to exactly half its current length. No new material. No summarising. Only deletion.

Then cut it in half again.

**Why this works:** The first cut removes the filler. The second cut removes what you thought was essential but wasn't. What remains is the story you were actually trying to tell — stripped of everything that protected you from telling it.

**Classroom note:** The second cut is where students get uncomfortable. That's the sign it's working. The story that survives both cuts is almost always better — and the student can see it.
